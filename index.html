Research Interests

I study why some (social) information is processed more than others. This prioritization sometimes diverges across groups, such as political partisans seeing the world differently, and is sometimes shared across people, such as some faces being more remembered than others. To study these questions, I primarily use computational methods combined with behavioral and neuroimaging experiments.

Related Publications

Natural language reveals that political partisans are more affectively aligned over political issues than partisan identities

2026

Rim, N., Jackson, J. C., Berman, M. G., & Leong, Y. C.

Communications Psychology, 4(1), 65.

We analyzed ~300 million Reddit comments and ~7 million news articles from partisan sources and found that partisan language is less affectively divided over political issues than over identity labels, suggesting meaningful affective alignment despite partisan animosity.

Differences in psychologists' cognitive traits are associated with scientific divides

2025

Sulik, J., Rim, N., Pontikes, E., Evans, J., & Lupyan, G.

Nature Human Behaviour, 9, 1147–1161.

Scientific disagreements are not just a matter of using different methods or having conflicting data: we surveyed 7,973 researchers in psychological sciences and found that disagreements are also associated with what they research and with their cognitive traits.

Natural scenes are more compressible and less memorable than human-made scenes

2025

Rim, N., Veillette, J., Lee, S. M., Kardan, O., Krishnan, S., Bainbridge, W. A., & Berman, M. G.

PsyArXiv

Across three behavioral experiments and a computational analysis of 108,754 scene images, we found that natural scenes contain less noticeable information than human-made scenes, making them more compressible, and that they are less memorable.
